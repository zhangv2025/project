DOES LIVING ALONE IMPAIR EMOTIONAL PERCEPTION?

A Data Storytelling Exploring Emotional Recognition in Solitude

Vanna Zhang





Overview - Middle Age

This project explores a counterintuitive hypothesis: Long-term solitary living may reduce a person’s ability to recognize emotions in others.

In a post-pandemic world where isolation has become more common—especially in dense cities or among young adults—the emotional cost of living alone is worth investigating.

While solitude is often romanticized as peaceful or independent, recent psychology and neuroscience research suggests that regular exposure to social cues might actually train and maintain our ability to read emotions.

The goal of this project is not to label solitude as “bad,” but to explore its subtle cognitive impact.

The proportion of one-person households in the U.S. has more than tripled from 7.7% in 1940 to 27.6% in 2020, highlighting a significant societal shift towards solitary living.





Emotion Recognition Accuracy

This chart compares the average emotion recognition accuracy between two groups: individuals who live alone and those who live in shared households.

The data indicates that people who live alone tend to score lower on facial emotion recognition tests, possibly due to reduced exposure to social cues in daily life.





Simulated Brain Activation

This simulated fMRI heatmap visualizes brain activity during emotional recognition tasks in response to mixed, positive, and negative stimuli.

Key emotional regions like the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex show varying degrees of activation between solitary and socially engaged individuals.

Simulated fMRI heatmap based on reference activation patterns in emotion recognition studies. Color gradient represents activation intensity.





Symbolic Illustration

This symbolic illustration contrasts emotional isolation with emotional engagement. The left side reflects stillness and disconnection, while the right side displays dynamic emotional reciprocity.

It’s not about morality. It’s about the experience of emotional mirroring.

Symbolic reconstruction based on emotional mirroring behavior studies.





Reflection

We often think of empathy as something we have. But maybe empathy is something we practice.

Without regular interaction, our emotional sensitivity may fade—subtly, but meaningfully.
