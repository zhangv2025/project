DOES LIVING ALONE IMPAIR EMOTIONAL PERCEPTION?

A DATA STORYTELLING PROJECT EXPLORING
EMOTIONAL RECOGNITION IN SOLITUDE

Vanna Zhang

Overview

This project explores a counterintuitive hypothesis:

Long-term solitary living may reduce a person’s ability to recognize emotions in others.

In a post-pandemic world where isolation has become more common—especially in dense cities or among young adults—the emotional cost of living alone is worth investigating.

While solitude is often romanticized as peaceful or independent, recent psychology and neuroscience research suggests that regular exposure to social cues might actually train and maintain our ability to read emotions.

The goal of this project is not to label solitude as “bad,” but to explore its subtle cognitive impact.

This data story uses a scroll-based visual narrative to guide viewers through that exploration—backed by real data, human insight, and emotional design.

Emotion Recognition Accuracy by Living Situation

This chart compares the average emotion recognition accuracy between two groups:

individuals who live alone and those who live in shared households.

The data indicates that people who live alone tend to score lower on facial emotion recognition tests, possibly due to reduced exposure to social cues in daily life.

While this doesn’t imply that everyone who lives alone becomes emotionally impaired, it raises a question:

Does our ability to read emotions depend on how often we’re socially engaged?

Simulated Brain Activation During Emotional Processing

This simulated fMRI heatmap visualizes the brain’s activation patterns during emotional recognition tasks across different emotional conditions.

The comparison focuses on individuals exposed to mixed, positive, and negative emotional stimuli, simulating cognitive activity for both socially connected and solitary groups.

Key emotional regions, such as the amygdala (Amg), hippocampus (Hipp), prefrontal cortex (PFC), and posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) show varying degrees of activation.

Notably, socially active participants (simulated) show increased activation in emotional and social cognition areas when processing emotional content, especially under mixed or ambiguous expressions.

This may suggest that social exposure helps preserve the brain’s sensitivity to nuanced emotional cues.

What Does Emotional Disconnection Look Like?

This hand-drawn illustration symbolically contrasts emotional isolation with emotional engagement.
